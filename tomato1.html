TOMATO PLANT DISEASES

1.Septoria Leaf Spot

Tomatoes are probably the most popular vegetable in the home garden. Unfortunately, they are also susceptible to Septoria leaf spot, the most common disease of tomatoes in Connecticut. Caused by the fungus Septoria lycopersici, this widespread disease is also called Septoria blight. It also includes a discussion of strategies to control and minimize the impact of this disease in backyard plantings.

Symptoms:

  • Small, circular brown spots with dark centers on the leaves.
  • Yellowing of the leaves surrounding the spots.
  • Affected leaves may develop numerous spots and eventually wither and die.
  • Lesions can enlarge and merge, forming larger irregular-shaped spots.
  • Lower leaves are usually affected first, with the disease progressing upward.

Precautions:

  • Avoid planting tomatoes or other Solanaceous crops in the same location for consecutive years. Rotate with non-host plants to break the disease cycle.
  • Provide adequate spacing between tomato plants to ensure good air circulation, which helps reduce humidity and minimize disease spread.
  • Water the plants at the base or use drip irrigation to keep the foliage dry. Avoid overhead watering, as wet leaves create a favorable environment for disease development.
  • Maintain healthy soil by incorporating organic matter, such as compost, to improve soil structure and enhance plant vigor.
  • Monitor tomato plants regularly for early signs of the disease, such as small spots on the leaves.

Recommendations:

  • Ensure proper spacing between plants for good air circulation.
  • Practice crop rotation to avoid consecutive plantings of tomatoes in the same area.
  • Water at the base of plants to keep foliage dry.
  • Prune infected leaves and remove plant debris to minimize disease spread.
  • Regularly monitor plants for early signs of disease and take prompt action.

Organic Control:

  • Disease-resistant Varieties:

    Choose tomato varieties that are resistant or tolerant to Septoria leaf spot. Check seed catalogs or consult local nurseries for recommended varieties

  • Crop Rotation:

    Rotate tomato plants with non-host crops to break the disease cycle. Avoid planting tomatoes or other Solanaceous plants in the same area for consecutive years.

  • Proper Plant Spacing:

    sufficient spacing between tomato plants to promote good air circulation, reducing humidity and creating a less favorable environment for disease development.

  • Pruning and Removal of Infected Leaves:

    Regularly prune lower branches and remove infected leaves to increase airflow and minimize the spread of the disease. Dispose of the infected plant material away from the garden.

  • Beneficial Insects and Predators:

    the presence of beneficial insects and predators, such as ladybugs or lacewings, that can feed on the fungal spores and help control the disease naturally.

2.Anthracnose

Anthracnose is a very common disease that can infect the leaves, fruit, and flowers of a large variety of plants. In tomatoes, it primarily infects fruit on the vine that are ripe or overripe.This disease is such a serious problem in processed tomato products that commercial processors adhere to a strict limit on how many lesions a tomato can have.

Symptoms:

  • Symptoms include dark, water-soaked lesions with a yellow halo on leaves.
  • Lesions can enlarge, turn brown or black, and coalesce.
  • Fruits develop sunken spots that become brown or black with a cracked texture.
  • Stems and petioles can have dark, sunken lesions, causing wilting or dieback.
  • Infected flowers wither and die, leading to reduced fruit set.

Precautions:

  • Practice crop rotation to avoid planting tomatoes in the same area each year.
  • Provide adequate spacing between tomato plants for good air circulation.
  • Avoid overhead watering; instead, water at the base of plants.
  • Remove and destroy any infected plant debris or fallen leaves.
  • Use clean gardening tools and equipment to prevent spreading the disease.

Recommendations:

  • Choose tomato varieties that are resistant to anthracnose.
  • Practice crop rotation, avoiding planting tomatoes in the same area for consecutive years.
  • Apply appropriate fungicides or biofungicides as recommended.
  • Provide adequate spacing between plants for good air circulation.
  • Remove and destroy infected plant debris to prevent disease spread.

Organic Control:

  • Disease-resistant Varieties:

    Choose tomato varieties that are resistant to anthracnose. Look for varieties specifically bred to have resistance to this fungal disease..

  • Crop Rotation:

    Practice crop rotation by avoiding planting tomatoes or other susceptible plants in the same area for consecutive years. This helps break the disease cycle and reduces the buildup of fungal spores in the soil.

  • Proper Plant Spacing and Air Circulation:

    Plant tomatoes with adequate spacing between plants to allow for good air circulation. This helps reduce humidity and creates an environment less favorable for the development and spread of anthracnose.

  • Mulching:

    Apply organic mulch, such as straw or shredded leaves, around tomato plants. Mulching helps prevent soil splashing, which can carry fungal spores onto the plants..

  • Organic Fertilizers and Soil Amendments:

    Use compost and organic fertilizers to promote healthy plant growth. Strong, healthy plants are more resistant to diseases, including anthracnose.

3.Fusarium Wilt

Fusarium wilt is a destructive fungal disease in tomatoes caused by Fusarium oxysporum. It infects the plant's vascular system, leading to wilting, stunted growth, and eventual plant death.Discoloration in the stem indicates the presence of the fungal infection. It is a soil-borne disease that restricts water and nutrient uptake, causing significant damage to tomato plants.

Symptoms:

  • Wilting: The affected tomato plants show wilting, starting from the lower leaves and progressing upwards. The leaves become limp and droopy.
  • Yellowing: Leaves of infected plants may exhibit yellowing or browning, especially in the lower part of the plant. The yellowing can gradually spread to the upper leaves.
  • Stunted Growth: Infected tomato plants experience stunted growth, failing to reach their expected height and size. They appear smaller and less vigorous compared to healthy plants.
  • Vascular Discoloration: When the stems of infected plants are cut open, brown or yellowish-brown discoloration can be observed in the vascular tissue. This discoloration indicates the presence of the Fusarium fungus, which has invaded and disrupted the plant's vascular system.

Precautions:

  • Choose resistant tomato varieties to Fusarium wilt..
  • Avoid planting in infested soil with a history of the disease.
  • Practice crop rotation with non-host crops.
  • Promptly remove and destroy infected plants, avoiding composting.

Recommendations:

  • Ensure proper soil drainage and avoid waterlogging.
  • Maintain good garden hygiene by removing and disposing of infected plant material.
  • Monitor plants regularly and take prompt action at the first signs of Fusarium wilt.
  • implement strict sanitation practices, including disinfecting tools and equipment between uses to prevent the spread of the Fusarium fungus.

Organic Control:

  • Soil Solarization:

    Before planting, cover the soil with clear plastic to trap heat from the sun, effectively sterilizing the soil and reducing Fusarium wilt pathogens.

  • Biocontrol Agents:

    Apply beneficial microorganisms, such as Trichoderma spp., to the soil or as seed treatments. These natural antagonists can suppress the growth of Fusarium fungi.

  • Compost and Organic Matter:

    Incorporate well-aged compost and organic matter into the soil to improve soil health and promote beneficial microbial activity, which can help suppress Fusarium wilt.

  • Crop Rotation with Non-Host Plants:

    tomato crops with non-host plants, such as beans, corn, or lettuce, to interrupt the disease cycle and reduce the population of Fusarium wilt in the soil.
