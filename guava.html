Guava Plant Diseases

1.Guava Wilt (Fusarium wilt)

Guava Wilt, caused by the fungus Fusarium oxysporum f. sp. psidii, is a devastating disease that affects guava plants. Here is some information on the symptoms, precautions, recommendations, and organic control methods for managing Guava Wilt

Remember, managing Guava Wilt can be challenging, and a combination of preventive measures and cultural practices is often necessary. It's recommended to seek guidance from local agricultural experts, extension services, or plant pathologists for specific recommendations tailored to your region and the severity of the disease. They can provide you with the most suitable organic control options and strategies to manage Guava Wilt effectively.

Symptoms:

  • Yellowing and Wilting: Infected guava plants exhibit yellowing and wilting of leaves, starting from the lower branches and progressing upward.
  • Leaf Drop: Leaves may drop prematurely, leading to defoliation of the plant.
  • Stunted Growth: Infected plants show stunted growth, with reduced vigor and overall decline.
  • Root Rot: The fungus attacks the root system, causing rotting and discoloration of the roots.

Precautions:

  • Plant Resistant Varieties: Choose guava varieties that are resistant or tolerant to Fusarium wilt.
  • Soil Management: Ensure well-drained soil and avoid waterlogging, as excessive moisture promotes the growth of the pathogen.
  • Sanitation: Remove and destroy any infected plant material, including fallen leaves and pruned branches.
  • Avoid Contaminated Tools: Disinfect pruning tools with a bleach solution between cuts to prevent the spread of the disease.

Recommendations:

  • Soil Amendments: Incorporate organic matter into the soil to improve soil structure and fertility, which can enhance plant health and resilience.
  • Fungicides: Some chemical fungicides may provide partial control of Guava Wilt. Consult with local agricultural experts or extension services for recommended fungicides and their application rates.
  • Enhancing Plant Health: Maintain proper plant nutrition through balanced fertilization and regular watering. Healthy plants are less susceptible to diseases.

Organic Control:

  • Organic Amendments:

    Apply well-decomposed compost or organic matter to improve soil health and enhance plant vigor.

  • Biological Control:

    Utilize biocontrol agents such as Trichoderma spp. or other beneficial microorganisms to suppress the growth of Fusarium fungi. Follow the recommended application rates and timings.

  • Resistant Varieties:

    Plant guava varieties that are naturally resistant or tolerant to Fusarium wilt.

  • Soil Management:

    Ensure proper drainage and avoid overwatering to minimize the conditions favorable for the pathogen's growth.

2.Guava Rust (Puccinia psidii)

Guava Rust, caused by the fungus Puccinia psidii, is a significant disease that affects guava plants. Here is some information on the symptoms, precautions, recommendations, and organic control methods for managing Guava Rust

It's important to note that managing Guava Rust may require a combination of strategies, and their effectiveness can vary depending on the severity of the disease and local conditions. It's recommended to consult with local agricultural experts, extension services, or plant pathologists for specific guidance and organic control options tailored to your region and the extent of Guava Rust infestation.

Symptoms:

  • Orange-yellow Pustules: Infected leaves, stems, and fruits develop raised, powdery orange-yellow pustules on their surfaces.
  • Leaf Deformation: Severe infections can cause leaf distortion, curling, and premature defoliation.
  • Stem Cankers: The fungus may also form cankers on the stems, leading to dieback and reduced vigor of the plant.

Precautions:

  • Plant Resistant Varieties: Select guava varieties that are resistant or tolerant to Guava Rust.
  • Sanitation: Remove and destroy any infected plant material, including fallen leaves, fruits, and pruned branches.
  • Avoid Overhead Irrigation: Moisture on the foliage promotes the spread of Guava Rust. Use irrigation methods that minimize leaf wetness, such as drip irrigation or soaker hoses.
  • Prevent Spread: Avoid movement of infected plant material and equipment between guava orchards or gardens.

Recommendations:

  • Fungicides: Chemical fungicides may be used to manage Guava Rust.
  • Pruning and Removal: Prune and remove severely infected branches or plant parts to reduce the disease's spread.
  • Weed Management: Control weeds, as they can serve as alternative hosts for Guava Rust and contribute to disease spread.

Organic Control:

  • Biological Control:

    Explore the use of beneficial microbes, such as Bacillus subtilis or Trichoderma spp., which can help suppress the growth of the rust fungus. These can be applied as foliar sprays or root drenches.

  • Copper-Based Products:

    Apply copper-based fungicides, such as copper hydroxide or copper sulfate, following label instructions. These can help suppress Guava Rust in organic farming practices.

  • Organic Amendments:

    Apply well-decomposed compost or organic matter to improve soil health and enhance plant vigor, which can make plants more resilient to diseases.

3.Algal leaf and fruit spot (Cephaleuros virescens Kuntze)

Algal leaf and fruit spot, caused by the pathogen Cephaleuros virescens Kuntze, is a common disease affecting various plants, including guava. Here is some information on the symptoms, precautions, recommendations, and organic control methods for managing Algal leaf and fruit spot.

It's important to note that algal leaf and fruit spot is primarily a cosmetic issue and rarely causes significant damage to the plant's overall health. However, if the infection is severe and affects a large portion of the plant, it can impact productivity. Regular monitoring, proper sanitation, and implementing suitable control measures can help manage the disease and minimize its impact on guava plants.

Symptoms:

  • Green Spots: Infected leaves and fruits develop greenish spots or patches on their surfaces. These spots may vary in size and shape.
  • Velvet-like Growth: The affected areas may also exhibit a velvety or fuzzy appearance due to the growth of the algal pathogen.
  • Reduced Photosynthesis: Severe infections can lead to a reduction in photosynthesis, affecting the overall growth and productivity of the plant.

Precautions:

  • Sanitation: Remove and destroy any infected plant material, including fallen leaves and fruits, to reduce the source of inoculum.
  • Avoid Overhead Irrigation: Moisture on the foliage promotes the growth and spread of the algal pathogen. Use irrigation methods that minimize leaf wetness, such as drip irrigation or soaker hoses.
  • Improve Air Circulation: Prune guava trees to enhance air movement and light penetration within the canopy, as it helps in reducing humidity and creating an unfavorable environment for the pathogen.
  • Avoid Excessive Fertilization: Excessive nitrogen fertilization can promote lush growth, making plants more susceptible to algal infections. Use balanced fertilization practices and avoid overfertilization.

Recommendations:

  • Use Disease-Free Planting Material: Start with healthy, disease-free mango seedlings or grafted trees from reputable sources.
  • Pruning: Regularly prune mango trees to remove infected branches and shoots showing signs of malformation.
  • Sanitation Practices: Practice good sanitation by removing and destroying any infected plant material, including infected branches, flowers, and fruit.

Organic Control:

  • Manual Removal:

    Regularly inspect the plants and manually remove any visible algal growth using a soft brush or sponge. Dispose of the removed material appropriately.

  • Plant Health Management:

    Maintain overall plant health through proper nutrition, watering, and balanced fertilization. Healthy plants are better equipped to withstand and recover from algal infections.
